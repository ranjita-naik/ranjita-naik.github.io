Start here: AI, LLMs, and agents — explained like you're smart but new

19 July 2026

This page assumes exactly nothing. Not what "LLM" stands for, not how ChatGPT works, nothing. If you're smart but new to all of this, you're the exact person it was written for. Ten minutes from now, the AI conversation happening around you will make sense.

1. The machine that finishes sentences

Start with something you already know: when your phone suggests the next word while you type, it's predicting what usually comes next. Now imagine that idea scaled up absurdly. Instead of learning from your texting habits, a system learns from a huge portion of everything humans have ever written — books, articles, code, conversations. That system is called a large language model, or LLM. ("Large" because of its scale, "language" because it works with text, "model" because it's a mathematical representation of patterns.)

Here's the surprising part, the part that even experts didn't fully expect: when you get good enough at predicting what text comes next, something that looks a lot like understanding emerges. To correctly finish the sentence "The doctor told the patient that the biopsy results were…", you need to grasp medicine, emotion, and how conversations work. Prediction at this scale forces competence. That's the whole trick — and it's why an LLM can draft your email, explain a contract, or write working computer code. ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini are all LLMs with a chat interface wrapped around them.

2. What the chatbot can't do

Talk to one of these chatbots and it feels capable of anything. But notice what's actually happening: it only produces words. Ask it "what's the cheapest flight to Delhi next month?" and it can tell you how to find out — it cannot go look. It has no hands. It can't open a website, check your calendar, read the file on your desktop, or send an email. It's a brilliant advisor locked in a room with no phone and no door, passing you notes.

For the first couple of years of this AI wave, that's what everyone used: a note-passing genius. Useful! But every actual action still fell to you.

3. Give it hands, and a loop

So engineers asked the obvious question: what if we let the words do things? The mechanism is simpler than you'd guess. You tell the LLM: "if you write a specially formatted note like search_flights(Delhi, March), our ordinary software will run that search and paste the results back to you." Those hookups are called tools. And then you add one more ingredient: instead of answering once and stopping, the system runs in a loop — look at the situation, decide the next small step, take it, look again — until the job is done.

An LLM plus tools plus a loop is called an agent. That's the word behind all the headlines, and now you know the entire recipe.

The entire difference between a chatbot and an agent, in one picture.

4. A concrete example

Tell an agent: "Find out how much we spent on software subscriptions last quarter and email me a summary." Watch what it does: it decides it needs data, uses a tool to list your files, spots the right spreadsheet, reads it, notices there's no total column, uses a calculator tool (LLMs are bad at arithmetic, and good agents know their own weaknesses), then sends the email — and, importantly, decides for itself that the job is finished. If a file is locked or a website is down, it reads the error and tries another route, the way a resourceful assistant would. You can watch precisely this scenario play out, step by step, in our interactive explainer — built for exactly the moment you finish this page.

5. Why everyone suddenly cares

Because "software you instruct in plain English, which then goes and does multi-step work" is a different economic object than "software that answers questions." Businesses are deploying agents for customer support, research, hiring pipelines, and coding — analysts at Gartner (via Firecrawl) project that a third of enterprise software will include agentic features by 2028. And because the field is this young, the gap between people who genuinely understand it and people who confidently talk about it is still wide — and you’ve now got the actual concept, not just the vocabulary.

6. Fair warnings

Agents fail in ways worth knowing about. They can be confidently wrong, and an agent that acts on a wrong belief does wrong things, not just wrong sentences. They cost real money — every "thought" is a paid computation. And giving software the ability to send emails or spend money means the boring engineering around permissions and checks matters more than the exciting part. Anyone who sells you agents without mentioning any of this is selling, not explaining.
