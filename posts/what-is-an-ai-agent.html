What actually is an AI agent? A plain-English guide

21 July 2026

You've heard the term in every product announcement and vendor pitch this year. Here's the simplest useful definition I've found:

A chatbot answers your question. An agent takes on your goal.

When you ask ChatGPT or Claude "how do I get a refund from this airline?", you get advice. That's a chatbot interaction: one question, one answer, and then it's your job to go do the thing. An agent is what you get when the AI can go do the thing itself — look up the booking, fill in the refund form, draft the complaint email, check back for a response — deciding step by step what to do next until the goal is reached or it needs your input.

The three ingredients

Strip away the jargon and almost every agent is a language model plus three capabilities. First, tools: the model can call things outside itself — search the web, read files, query a database, send an email. Second, memory and state: it keeps track of what it has already done and what it learned along the way, so step five can build on step two. Third, a loop: instead of answering once and stopping, it repeatedly observes the situation, decides on the next action, acts, and checks the result. That loop is the "agentic" part — it's what lets the system recover when step three fails and try a different route.

That's it. When a vendor says "agentic AI," a good mental test is to ask: what tools can it use, what does it remember, and what happens when a step fails? If the answers are vague, you're probably looking at a chatbot in a trench coat.

Why is everyone suddenly talking about this?

Two things changed. The models got reliable enough at multi-step reasoning to be trusted with sequences of actions, not just single answers. And the plumbing got standardized — protocols like MCP (Model Context Protocol) give agents a common way to connect to tools and data, the way USB gave devices a common way to connect to computers. The result is that agents moved from research demos to production surprisingly fast: Gartner (via Firecrawl) projects that a third of enterprise software will include agentic AI by 2028, and that by 2029 the majority of routine customer service issues will be resolved without a human touching them.

What agents are actually good at today

The real picture is jagged. Agents shine where the work is made of verifiable steps — code, data processing, research and summarization, structured workflows like triaging support tickets or screening applications — because at each step there's a way to check whether the step worked. They struggle where success is fuzzy, stakes are high, and mistakes are hard to detect. That's why the first wave of real deployments is concentrated in software development, customer support, and back-office workflows, not in open-ended judgment calls.

Questions worth asking before you build (or buy) one

If you're a business owner wondering whether this matters for you yet, skip the technology and start with the workflow. Look for a task your team does repeatedly that follows a recognizable pattern but carries too much variation for old-school automation. Check that you can describe what "done correctly" looks like clearly enough for a system to verify its own work. And weigh the cost of the agent getting it wrong once — a minor annoyance is survivable, a fired customer is not. Tasks that are repetitive, pattern-shaped, checkable, and low-blast-radius when they fail are where agents earn their keep today. Everything else can wait, and waiting is a legitimate strategy.
